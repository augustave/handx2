The Hand of God Problem  
Why Product Designers Need to Learn National Security

In 1986, Diego Maradona scored the most infamous goal in football history. Argentina vs. England. World Cup quarterfinal. The ball dropped into the box, Maradona rose, and it went in—off a hand the referee didn't see. Later he called it La Mano de Dios: the Hand of God.

People still argue about that moment like it's theology. Was it genius? Theft? Destiny? But the interesting question isn't the morality. It's why it became a national myth.

I learned that the hard way. First day of school in Buenos Aires, where my father had just been posted as a diplomat. I had maybe two sentences of Spanish, and recess was a wall of noise I couldn't parse. A kid walked up to me and asked—in words I could barely decode but whose meaning was unmistakable—whether Maradona's goal was valid. Was I pro-English, or else?

I had nothing. No Spanish. No Maradona. No idea a goal could be a theological event. But to them, this was a rite of passage. It wasn't a debate about rules. It was a belonging test.

(The late Pope Francis—a die-hard San Lorenzo fan—would have been hard-pressed to call it a foul. It might be the only lie a Pope could tell as God's representative on earth: that the goal was legitimate.)

Argentina was living in the afterburn of the Falklands/Malvinas War. Defeat, humiliation, grief. England wasn't just an opponent; it was the symbol of a recent victor. In that context, the Hand of God wasn't experienced as a petty rule-break. It was revenge. A reset. Not clean, not polite—effective. Proof that the strong don't always get to decide the rules. When it went in, church bells tolled, car horns blared, strangers wept in each other's arms. It wasn't just a goal. It was a ritual—and I mean that in a spiritual sense—to take out a curse.

The uncomfortable insight isn't "cheating is good." It's that people unify around capability and will—especially when official channels feel rigged against them. A shared myth can work like a cultural immune response: it compresses complexity into a story that restores agency. That schoolyard test—you must be pro-country, no matter what—was part of a nation's immune system against anything that would break it further.

I'm a product designer, and that mythology keeps resurfacing when I look at American tech right now. Not because we should cheat. Not because ethics are optional. Because we're stuck in a trance—mistaking weakness for virtue while the world moves on without us.

The Trance of Performative Goodness  
There's a peculiar reflex in American tech culture: the faster someone builds something powerful, the more urgently they perform uncertainty about whether it should exist at all.

Build frontier AI? Immediately signal worry, apologize for the implications, hope you don't "dominate."  
Build critical infrastructure? Talk like you're an accidental bystander to power, not a steward of it.  
Control a chokepoint in the stack? Flinch. Downplay. Apologize for leverage.  
Caution is wisdom. Performative caution is something else. It's narrating your own self-handicapping as morality—treating defenselessness as virtue and calling it "principle."

There's a saying: a good man is a very dangerous man who has that under voluntary control. The tech sector has inverted it. We built extraordinary power, then refused to acknowledge it, secure it, or wield it. That's not virtue. That's leaving loaded weapons in unlocked rooms and calling it peace.

Game theorists have a name for where this ends. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, the worst outcome isn't mutual conflict. It's the Sucker's Payoff: you cooperate while the other side defects. Open research norms, performative caution, and universal trust on one side; industrial espionage, state subsidies, and total mobilization on the other. It feels virtuous from the inside. Strategically, it's a donation.

The Two-Part Attack We're Only Defending Against Half Of  
Here's what most people miss: a serious intelligence operation has at least two components. Tech, and PSYOP.

We can field the best cyberwarfare units in the world. We can run the most advanced AI labs. We can win the technology side completely—and still lose, because the second component targets something no firewall covers: a society's willingness to defend its own advantage.

When DeepSeek released a competitive model, watch the discourse: mainstream commentators and YouTubers all but cheering for the American labs to lose. Maybe that was organic. But if you were designing a PSYOP to tank a rival's markets and seed doubt in its builders, it would look exactly like that.

And the other side isn't hiding the playbook. The Pentagon's 2025 Annual Report to Congress describes Beijing's vision of future conflict not as a clash of militaries but as a "clash of national systems"—a whole-of-nation mobilization it calls "national total war," folding civilian, economic, and technological resources into the fight. Congressional investigators and independent analysts estimate DeepSeek built its models on a stockpile of roughly 50,000 Hopper-generation Nvidia GPUs accumulated around export-control deadlines—and PLA entities referenced DeepSeek models in at least a dozen military procurement tenders in 2025\. The same Pentagon report notes the PLA Navy keeps training with civilian roll-on/roll-off commercial vessels to cover its shortfall in amphibious landing ships. In a national-systems framework, a private lab hoarding compute is indistinguishable from a navy hoarding oil.

We are building products in a market. They are building capacity in a system. When those two models collide, the market usually loses.

The simplest way to beat a strong country is to convince it to sabotage itself.  
The Sword You Pretend Not to Hold  
If you're designing communication infrastructure, cloud platforms, AI systems, social networks, supply chain software—anything that shapes coordination at scale—you are not "just" building a product. You're building dual-use infrastructure. Whether you like the label or not.

The boundary between "civilian tech" and "national security" didn't dissolve because anyone signed a treaty. It dissolved because software became the terrain.

Let me put this in product terms, since that's the language we actually speak:

Asset: frontier AI \+ cloud \+ comms platforms  
Attack surface: polarization, talent pipelines, open-research defaults, supply chain opacity  
Failure mode: capability diffusion \+ narrative corrosion \+ internal non-coordination  
Controls: operational security, selective openness, standards leadership, clear Valley↔DC interfaces  
Success metric: time-to-innovation advantage maintained without legitimacy collapse  
This isn't abstract. A frontier AI lab carries more strategic weight than most traditional defense contractors. A social platform can shift a country's information environment. A semiconductor design team sits on a global constraint. China controls roughly 90% of the world's rare-earth processing—the refining and metal-making that turns rock into components—and a single F-35 contains over 900 pounds of rare-earth materials. Supply chains are the battlefield now.

So the question isn't whether product designers are part of the national security architecture. We already are. The question is whether we understand the threat models and failure modes of what we're building.

Right now, most of us don't. Not because we're stupid. Because the culture trains us not to look. There's a script: building powerful systems is suspicious; securing those systems is paranoid; coordinating on threats is "militaristic." Meanwhile—default-open research norms, casual operational hygiene, soft talent pipelines, weak assumptions about adversaries.

That's a posture that assumes the world is non-adversarial, at the exact moment the world is getting more adversarial. Ethics require recognizing intent. Treating hostile and friendly actors as morally equivalent isn't sophistication. It's negligence dressed as purity.

Ideological Subversion as an Attack Surface (Use Carefully)  
The old Cold War phrase "ideological subversion" comes with baggage, so I'll use it narrowly: adversaries don't only attack systems. They attack a society's confidence in its right to defend itself.

This is not a new discovery. In 1955, Edward Bernays—with Howard Walden Cutler writing its structural chapters—published The Engineering of Consent, the cold thesis that in a mass-media society, public opinion doesn't just happen; it gets engineered, deliberately, by people who define objectives and map themes. Mass media had turned the country into a small room where one voice could be amplified a millionfold. Silicon Valley has since built a room smaller, louder, and more volatile than Bernays could have imagined. We engineered the room. We're too embarrassed to admit we're engineers.

The mechanics are mundane. Push stories that make capability feel shameful. Amplify cynicism. Weaponize internal fractures. You don't need traitors. You need incentives plus narrative gravity—a culture conditioned, as the defector Yuri Bezmenov described it, to look at evidence and shrug.

To be precise about the line: healthy critique is not subversion. Governance questions are real. AI-safety risks are real. Concentrated power is a legitimate concern. The line is between accountability and self-disarmament—between "we should steer this responsibly" and "we should feel guilty for having it."

When a culture can't tell the difference, it becomes easy to steer.

What Unity Actually Looks Like Now  
I watched the inauguration and saw, in one frame, a cluster of tech leaders—Bezos, Zuckerberg, Pichai, Musk, Cook, Altman—gathered near the center of American political power.

My father worked Protocol in the Foreign Service. Seating charts were his chess. So I know something most commentators don't: positions at an inauguration aren't made off-the-cuff. They're composed—for foreign governments—the way a sentence is composed. The guest list is a forward indicator of policy. Putting that particular stack of CEOs in the frame was a message: AI is a state priority now. It was flexing toward Beijing and Moscow. It was the equivalent of parading a new tank through Red Square.

The immediate reaction from many: corruption, oligarchy, dystopia.

Mine was different—not because billionaires are saints; they're not. It's a habit I picked up from watching professionals read a room: look past the user interface of politics to the code underneath. The UI of that frame was wealth standing too close to power. The code was capacity—the leaders of the systems that shape global communication, commerce, cloud, AI research, and data flows, assembled in one shot. Russia can parade tanks. China can stage industrial spectacle. That image represented the capacity to design, build, deploy, and coordinate at planetary scale.

That so many Americans can only read their own national system as shame—that's the vulnerability. If your culture treats its strongest capabilities as moral stains by default, you don't need foreign enemies. You've already done their work.

Unity in 2026 isn't chants. It's interfaces. Not vibes—protocols:

Shared threat models between government and tech  
Clear dual-use governance frameworks that work in practice  
Export controls that are both enforceable and legitimate  
Rapid coordination patterns for emerging threats  
Supply-chain security treated as a design constraint, not an afterthought  
Standards-setting strategies that advantage allied ecosystems  
Operational security as routine, not cosplay  
This doesn't require turning Silicon Valley into the Pentagon. It requires admitting reality: we build power. Then we decide—intentionally—how it's secured, governed, and used. And if we don't decide, someone else will. Regulators, adversaries, or crisis.

The Education Gap (My Actual Point)  
I'm a product designer. And I've realized something basic:

Product designers need national security literacy, because we are already assets in a national security architecture—whether we admit it or not. Not "should be." Already are.

We need to understand how real intelligence services operate, not the Hollywood version. Supply chain risk and hardware compromise. Information operations and narrative warfare. Counterintelligence as operational hygiene. The difference between "open by default" and "strategically open." How product choices create geopolitical leverage—or hand it away.

Here's the part that should make this feel less foreign: this is the skill we already have, pointed at a domain we've been avoiding. My first CPO—my father—trained me for it without ever using the words. Discomfort? Good. Ambiguity? Good. Pressure? Good. Product design is the discipline of sitting with messy, adversarial, ambiguous systems without flinching—and shipping anyway. That is exactly the temperament national security work requires. The gap isn't aptitude. It's literacy.

This isn't partisan. Swap the parties, swap the names, swap the flags—the point holds: if you build infrastructure, you inherit adversaries. The goal isn't paranoia. The goal is adult realism. Comfort is not a strategy.

The Hand of God Is Not an Instruction Manual  
So what does Maradona's hand have to do with any of this?

It's not that America should cheat. The Hand of God isn't a playbook. It's a metaphor for recognizing the game you're actually in.

Annie Jacobsen—who spent years interviewing the CIA's paramilitary legends for Surprise, Kill, Vanish—tells a story about Billy Waugh and Ric Prado. She asked each of them: if you two had to kill each other, who would win? Of course, each said "me." Then Billy came back to her: "Let me tell you how I would win. I'd cheat. I'd show up before the duel and I'd kill him."

That's not about murder. It's about a mindset. The dangerous player isn't the one preparing for a fair duel. It's the one who refuses the duel entirely—who changes the terrain, shows up early, flips the script, and wins before you recognize what game you're in. Some rivals will use your openness against you. Some will copy your work while mocking your hesitation. Some will weaponize narratives to keep you arguing with yourself while they build. Respond only with polite predictability, and you've done half their job for them.

Strategic maturity looks like this: build capability without apology. Practice operational security as discipline. Choose openness from strength, not habit. Coordinate where it reduces risk, without surrendering autonomy. Treat governance as part of the product, not an external punishment.

Two guardrails, because this is where the argument usually goes off the rails.

First: strategic cunning without ethics is just corruption in a nicer suit. But the inverse is equally true—ethics without strategic cunning is an elaborate form of surrender.

Second—and Argentina teaches this too—myth-mode can't be permanent. La Mano de Dios sentiment can carry a nation through an emergency; it cannot govern one. A people, or an industry, can rally around a myth. Long-term strength comes from institutions, competence, and legitimacy. The myth can spark. It can't steer.

Strength Precedes Virtue  
There's a basic truth that makes modern people uncomfortable: restraint only means something if you're capable of doing otherwise. He who has a sword, and knows how to use it, but keeps it sheathed—he inherits the earth.

American tech built the sword. Now comes the harder part: admitting we're holding it, learning when to keep it sheathed and when to draw it, and refusing to hand it to people who will use it against us.

The United States didn't get here by accident, and not by staying comfortable. The 160th Special Operations Aviation Regiment—the Night Stalkers—was born from catastrophic failure: Operation Eagle Claw, Iran, 1980\. Eight Americans dead in the desert. National humiliation. We didn't hide from that failure. We studied it, rebuilt from it, and created the most capable special-operations aviation unit on earth. China's military hasn't fought a major war since 1979\. That crucible—failing, adapting, executing—cannot be stockpiled. There is no substitute for expertise forged in actual failure. Failure? Good.

If the United States keeps treating its own capability as suspect by default—if it never integrates technological leadership with coherent strategy—the decline won't feel like a dramatic collapse. It will feel like Rome: slow, avoidable, then sudden when it's too late to reverse.

As the old Rabbinic dictum warns: "He who is compassionate to the cruel will ultimately become cruel to the compassionate."

Perhaps these are grand conclusions for complex times. Which brings me to the final line—and it's not modesty. It's the indictment.

I am just a product designer.

If I can see that we're building dual-use infrastructure while pretending it's "just tech," then none of us has an excuse not to learn the literacy required to steward it.

The trance ends when we accept the sword in our hands.
